Abstract

Dot Matrix printers are one of the first forms of printers to be created and they utilize physical stamping of soaked ink sheets by the machine onto paper to create small dots. Many of these dots in a line and other configurations will create the characters used to create text documents and other such physical mediums. The introduction of Dot Matrix printers had a huge impact on Typographic art which is the use of numbers and letters to create artwork. One example of this is how ASCII art was made more accessible for the general public which made the creative boundaries broaden in this field. Typographic art in general

One of the most recognizable forms of typographic art is called ASCII art. According to #asciiville# it is ‘a graphic design technique that uses computers for presentation and consists of pictures pieced together from the 95 printable (from a total of 128) characters defined by the 1963 ASCII Standard. Some Ascii art uses ASCII compliant character sets with proprietary extended set of characters (beyond the 128 characters of standard 7-bit ASCII).’ However it also mentions that it refers to ASCII art also being a more encompassing term for most art created using text, so in some manner of speaking, ACSII art and typographic art are one and the same. There were three main reasons that ACSII art first arose, first of all the printers at the time ‘lacked graphics ability and thus characters were used in place of graphic marks.’, this technological limit is also the reason many other art forms in history arose. Secondly, ‘ASCII art was also used in early e-mail when images could not be embedded’. And thirdly it was used not just out of how it looked or to please the eye but it had more of a practical use being that it would mark divisions between different print jobs so that the change could be spotted more easily.

The lifetime of typographic art has seen it through an evolution of changes. The invention of Dot matrix Printers

Fritz Karl Preikschat is the sole inventor of the dot matrix printer. ‘From 1952 until 1954, Fritz Karl Preikschat submitted five patents for his invention of a teleprinter with seven print wires for a 7×5 dot matrix’, however due to preexisting technology and a lack of demand, the idea was shown no interest by his current employers. This is one of the many reasons that the invention of the printer is attributed instead to Robert howard more often times as he was the first person to create a practical working version whilst employed at Epson. One other contributor to the invention was Yasuki Kawai who was instrumental in further development whilst working at Epson.
